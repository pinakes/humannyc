Human.nyc began with a broad goal of helping street homeless folks.

We asked people what they needed they weren’t often receiving. They talked to us about the need for socks, tampons and pads, and winter coats. So, we partnered with other businesses and nonprofits to receive those items, we build a team of volunteers to distribute them, and we hit the streets.

It didn’t take long before our conversations with folks reached a deeper level.

Concerning trends began to arise: people told us they felt safer on the streets than in the shelters and about the bureaucracy they faced before being offered any form of housing.

Our team felt compelled to switch gears, shifting our focus from distributing items to policy and advocacy. But our methodology never changed. It was and always will be about listening to people who are homeless. Put simply, they know homelessness better than anyone else.

We learned how to ask the right questions to really get at the nuances of what was keeping folks on the streets. We identified the shelters that were driving people away from the shelter system, we learned about a process where people who already feel invisible need to be “sighted” multiple times to get a case manager, and we heard how difficult it is to meet the city’s 9-months of living on the streets requirement for transitional or permanent housing.

After two years of hearing people’s stories, we have identified the gaps between how the system was supposed to work and how people are experiencing it, and compiled the evidence we needed to make a compelling case for reform.

We’ve now launched Human.nyc, an advocacy resource for anyone looking to help our street homeless neighbors secure housing.

Please do not hesitate to reach out to us at hello@human.nyc

Step One

Become Street Homeless

Overview

People need to live on the streets nine months to meet the city’s “chronicity requirement.” Until reaching that point, and working with outreach teams to prove it, the majority of housing programs, transitional or permanent, are not options.

In order to be eligible for a Safe Haven, a person must meet, and provide a way to prove they meet, the city’s chronicity requirement. This provides a barrier to many people who have recently become homeless or left the shelter system, leading to a wait of up to nine months before becoming eligible for transitional housing. In practice, we have found that people who are working with a case manager (see step 2) are often in limbo; people experiencing street homeless want to move into transitional housing but have to wait for one of two things (sometimes both): they become eligible or a vacancy becomes available. Step Two

Sighting Process

A person no longer has a place to stay and begins to live on the street full time.

Overview

People need to live on the streets nine months to meet the city’s “chronicity requirement.” Until reaching that point, and working with outreach teams to prove it, the majority of housing programs, transitional or permanent, are not options.

Street homeless New Yorkers often leave the shelter system after poor experiences during the intake and assessment process. Intake and assessment take place at shelters that have over 200 residents at a time, and people often report that the conditions are especially poor at these shelters. Intake and assessment is supposed to take 21 days, although it normally takes longer. Afterwards, residents will be transferred to a program shelter, which is likely smaller.

Theft, violence, and expired/ unhealthy food in shelters are cited as some of the top reasons people choose not to stay in shelters, and instead feel safer or more comfortable sleeping on the sidewalks of New York City. People tell us that when they sleep on the streets, they get to choose who they sleep around and have less fear that their belongings will disappear overnight. Some choose to not even try the shelters after hearing other people’s experiences and reading about the crime and violence within the system.

In his Turning the Tide on Homelessness report, the Mayor recognized that historically people in shelters have been placed in neighborhoods and boroughs far from where they came from - he pledged to work on placing people in shelters closer to their communities of origin. However, we continue to encounter single adults whose assigned shelter is located far away from the services and people they want to be near; people continue to choose sleeping on the streets near their daily programs, like outpatient substance abuse treatment, rather than commute hours each day from their shelter.

Recommendations

Below, we have proposed a series of options for people who do not yet meet the 9-month chronicity requirement. The city should do everything in its power to avoid forcing folks to live outside for 9 months while waiting for placement.

The strict eligibility for adult families (families without children) at AFIC, the Adult Family Intake Center, is a driver in street homelessness. There is no right to shelter for adult families who want to stay together, and instead, adult families must go through intake and eligibility process which often ends in an “ineligible for shelter” determination. Many people prefer to stay outside with their loved ones than separate into different shelters.

People describe pets as members of their family, and choose to stay connected with their pets rather than give them up to live in a shelter. To access shelters and other resources (such as medical clinics, inpatient treatment, soup kitchens, etc.) they are told they must separate from their pet, a decision they may not choose, therefore leaving them few options but to remain unsheltered and untreated.

After the assessment process, each individual gets "categorized" and placed into a program shelter. While, in theory, assessments and placements into a program shelter should be individualized, we have learned that there are very low thresholds to place someone into mental health, substance abuse, or dual-diagnoses (MICA) shelters. For example, people who report seeking mental health services for anxiety, despite holding down a job, may be categorized as a “mental health client” and placed into a program shelter for people with high mental health needs, rather than in an employment shelter where there is further support around employment and independent living options. Similarly, a person who reports smoking marijuana one time per month would be placed into a substance abuse shelter.
