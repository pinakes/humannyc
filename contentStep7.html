Overview

There is not enough housing for people experiencing homelessness. The bureaucracy described in steps 1-6 is a byproduct of the city needing to ration scarce housing. Addressing this gap is the only way to truly end homelessness.

The most recent data has suggested that there is only one unit of supportive housing for every four eligible people in need of housing, highlighting the lack of vacant units available for vulnerable homeless New Yorkers. In 2014, the length of time from obtaining an approved supportive housing application to an acceptance into supportive housing was over a year for individuals, likely due to the lack of available capacity.

In addition to few available supportive housing units, there continues to be a shortage of market rate apartments available to extremely low, very low, and low income households. In 2018, extremely low income households had incomes less than $21,930 for one person, very low income households had incomes up to $36,550 for one person, and low income households had incomes up to $58,480 for one person.

Despite legislative efforts to protect voucher holders, the Human Rights Law expanded in 2008 to protect voucher holders has proven difficult to enforce. The size and scope of New York City’s housing market and a lack of public knowledge about the law enables widespread source of income discrimination. At Neighbors Together, members have had their voucher for 2 to 3 years, and remain homeless, unable to find a landlord willing to accept the program.

Recommendations

New York City’s housing market presents a series of tremendous challenges. Fortunately, there are dozens of phenomenal organizations advocating for increasing housing stock through common sense reforms. The solutions exist, but we need to build urgency for them.

Per the Coalition For The Homeless, “New York City is in the midst of the worst homelessness crisis since the Great Depression, with 63,000 people – including more than 23,000 children – sleeping in shelters each night. In order to match the unprecedented need, the City must employ every available affordable housing resource. Mayor de Blasio must align his historic Housing New York plan with the reality of record homelessness by immediately increasing the total number of housing units created for homeless households to 30,000 – 10 percent of his overall 300,000-unit plan."

In a 2007 survey, Picture The Homeless “found 1,723 abandoned buildings—containing 11,170 apartments—and 505 vacant lots in Manhattan. If those apartments were renovated and housing were built on the vacant lots, the group says, it would provide 24,000 units—which could become homes for the more than 9,000 families and 7,000 single adults living in city shelters last April, the 4,000 people the city says are living on the street, and another 4,000 street homeless who were likely missed in the city’s count.”

We support VOCAL-New York’s recommendation that New York City accelerate its timeline for the creation of the promised 15,000 units of permanent supportive housing from 15 years to 10 years. Likewise, we support VOCAL-New York’s recommendation that the state fully fund and create 20,000 units of supportive housing. Governor Cuomo promised 20,000 units and released funding for the first 6,000 in April, 2017.

Both Picture The Homeless and VOCAL-NY, among other organizations, have advocated for further utilization of the Our Space Initiative. Per Picture The Homeless’ Business of Homelessness report, the city should “Shift DHS capital funding away from the expansion of the shelter system, and reinvest these capital funds into subsidies for extremely low income and homeless households. If $80 million in capital funds were shifted each year from DHS to HPD, the city could nearly double the amount currently invested in the Our Space Initiative, which subsidizes up to 30% of units in a new construction project with rents at the current shelter allowance.”

"Neighbors Together, working alongside VOCAL-NY, and Partnership for the Homeless in the New York City Homeless Union (NYCHU), compiled a series of recommendations to increase the City’s effectiveness at fighting source of income discrimination, and shared them with Commissioner Banks at the Department of Social Services.

As part of the new CityFHEPs voucher program, we agree with Council Member Levin’s crucial recommendation that the voucher amount be increased for all household levels. He states, “The current voucher amount is simply too low to meet the cost of housing in New York. We need to commit to a process that meets rental needs for all households facing homelessness."

The Medicaid Redesign Team (MRT) housing subsidy is a New York State Medicaid funded supportive housing program that began in 2013. The MRT program targets high-cost Medicaid recipients with the goal of reducing preventable hospitalizations and emergency health care costs by providing housing coupled with health care case management. Several studies have shown that addressing the social determinants of health, such as housing, dramatically decreases health care costs, particularly for homeless single adults.
