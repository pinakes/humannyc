Overview

This step describes the process of applying for all of the vital documents someone needs to move forward in the housing process. Outreach teams do not always the funds to cover the cost of vital documents, leaving homeless New Yorkers with the difficult task of paying for these themselves.

Individuals must have a social security card to prove their identity and citizenship and to show eligibility for many types of housing, including Section 8 and supportive housing. A person is required to at least have a copy of their social security card when applying for housing and the original may be requested depending on program and housing type. This process can be difficult for a number of reasons.

Birth certificates are another way which people must prove their identity to move forward in the housing process. Many times, a copy is not acceptable and an original must be provided. People often encounter difficulties in obtaining their birth certificate, particularly those born out of state.

A prerequisite for permanent housing is an income. In New York City, this income can take the form of employment, but more frequently for individuals experiencing homelessness, it takes the form of Public Assistance, Supplemental Security Income (SSI), or Social Security Disability Income (SSDI). One part of Public Assistance is Cash Assistance, which can be used just like cash to pay for everyday living expenses.

People are required to have a form of photo identification to obtain permanent housing. For individuals who need to obtain a new state ID, the process of waiting at the DMV can be stressful. In order to obtain a state ID, individuals must be able to bring original versions of their birth certificate and social security card to their DMV appointment.

People who have served in the United States military may be eligible for services through the Veterans Administration, including access to the HUD-VASH program, a supportive housing program which combines a Housing Choice Voucher with supportive services provided by the Veterans Administration. Veterans must provide a copy of their DD-214 to be considered for this program.

Unfortunately, there are numerous barriers that people experiencing homelessness encounter during the process of obtaining critical forms of identification and documents. There are a limited number of offices all across New York that assist with identification and documentation and people often have to return mulitple times before all requirements are met.

Recommendations

Our recommendations provide ways to reduce barriers to obtaining documentation and benefits needed to move forward in the housing process. The city has effective services which we recommend be enhanced and continued, and we also advocate for new policies to better serve folks experiencing street homelessness.

We recommend that outreach team contracts have funding specifically to pay for vital documents. Currently, there are limited options for service providers to pay for vital documents for street homeless individuals. A few organizations, like the Coalition for the Homeless, are able to pay for certain documents, although there are income limits associated with these services. Finding the $17.75 for a replacement birth certificate in New York City should not be a barrier to moving forward in the housing process.

The Department of Social Services/Human Resources Administration (DSS/HRA) mandates a “Duty to Assist” policy at Job Centers and Supplemental Nutrition Assistance Program (SNAP) Centers. Among other things, this policy helps people who were born out of state to secure their documentation. We believe this program, which no street homeless folks we have spoken to have heard of, needs to be better advertised to ensure that people born out of state are aware of it.

We believe HRA should increase the amount of cash assistance provided to people in shelter and other transitional settings to better cover the cost of living and basic essentials. Unfortunately, although programs often try to (and are expected to) provide carfare to and from appointments, meals and snacks, and toiletries, these essentials are often not provided to individuals in transitional housing services. Furthermore, people in shelters have reported bathrooms being consistently out of toilet paper, and meals being simply inedible, necessitating the need for people to buy these items themselves.
